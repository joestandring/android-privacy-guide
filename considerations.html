Considerations

Information

While I have taken as much care as I can and have researched the advice and software in this guide in-depth, there remains a chance that some information may be inaccurate. This is an independent project with no funding for research. If you see something you do not believe to be accurate or if you have any other queries regarding this guide, please make a pull request/issue on this sites GitHub repo or Email me.

Compromise

When using a mobile device connected to a cellular network your location can be tracked, including by IMSI-catcher devices that can spoof cell tower signals to connect devices to it and provide a physical location. This means that this guide will not protect you from tracking by government agencies and therefore is not suitable if state surveillance is a part of your threat model.

Responsibility

By following some steps in this guide you risk damaging your device in a potentially unrecoverable way. While this is very unlikely if you follow steps carefully, I take no responsibility for any damage caused.

Balancing privacy and useability

Before proceeding you should consider how important privacy is for you personally. This guide is not meant to convince you one way or another, however it is important to understand that taking steps to increase privacy with any software can reduce useability in cases. Being thorough is the most effective way to secure your privacy however this guide is structured in a way that you can pick and choose the changes you want depending on your preference.

Alternatives

Pinephone

~$150 smartphone capable of running several mobile and desktop Linux distros. Kill switches for modem, WiFi, microphones, and both cameras separately. While many distros look promising and run stable, Android apps are not supported meaning a major loss in useability. This will likely get better as more people get their hands on them.

Librem 5

~$750 smartphone with the same use case and advantages/problems as the PinePhone. Fewer kill switches (modem, WiFi, linked cameras and audio) but slightly better specs overall.

iOS

While nowhere near as extensive as a hardened Android install, iOS is better than a stock Android device in terms of privacy. Google is a company who's revenue comes from targeted advertisements that use user data whereas Apple profits mainly from hardware sales. It should also be kept in mind that iOS is closed source, meaning that the legitimacy of many of their claims when it comes to privacy are not able to be proved entirely.
